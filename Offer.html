Offer

Back to home 9 May 2026

    It feels like I’m floating. I’ve convinced myself that I am. My head’s hanging off of the bed: blood pooling, head tightening, hair dangling—I don’t have long. The cold sun shines through my dorm window, and I let my vision drown in that refractive haze.

    Last night I got my junior summer offer at Centerview. I haven’t told anyone yet. It was late. I was alone on my couch when it came in. Flowers in my ribcage when I saw the email’s notification. I let it sit there for about twenty seconds after I saw it. Then I tapped.

    There it all was. The last two years of all that accounting, those statements, fucking valuation. The coffee chats. The interviews. I had it all right there, back of the head, and I spoke the language. Banking was never going to be as hard as any class, any stats or econ or even the old high school history (which had proved a challenge at the time). But it was beautiful, though. That night, immediately post-offer, a cone of lamp-illuminated dust had caught my gaze. I really had done it. I was set. I am set. For a moment I had put down my phone and just stared off out the window, seeing the lights from Harvard Square glinting through amidst that darkened, darkened gray all around. I had thought about today (tomorrow then), about Grace, about home, high school—it really wasn’t that hard then. Classes were mostly a joke, and applying to college could be a moderate assignment these days. It really wasn’t that hard getting in here. But it was all I wanted. Centerview was all I wanted. I wonder what Grace is up to.

    “What the fuck are you doing?” My roommate stood just inside my doorframe.

    “Hi Lily,” I replied.

    “How long have you been lying like that?”

    “Maybe a little bit.”

    “I just finished getting ready. Let’s go in the next few.”

    She closed my door slightly too sharply. I had the impression that my hair looked magnificent just dangling there hanging off my head, like long vines, like one of those willows in the Public Garden. I let the image stay suspended in my mind for a moment longer, and I rolled off the bed.


    Lily, Caden, James, and I were going to Boston for the afternoon. We planned to meet up with my friend from home on Newbury, near BU (she goes there), and then walk and shop and drink boba until we got to Chinatown, where we’d eat dinner at this Sichuan restaurant I really like. It was to be a proper day on the town, and for me, a celebration of sorts. I hadn’t seen Grace for a few months; of course, we’d call every few days but I just thought about the last time we were home on break sitting outside the cafe by the train station when it was too damn cold to be sitting outside, talking about it all: me, leaning back too far in my chair and her, sitting prim with that air of clarity that always seemed to follow her. The semester had been busy. I was excited to see her again. I wonder what she’d think of Caden.

—

    “Hey Renee.” Caden scooped himself off of one of the Harvard Square benches and strided towards us. He had long strides. He was tall. “Hey Lily.” Probably a little taller than me. “How’s it been?” he said, pushing his hair away from his eyes.

    “It’s been,” I said. “But I got my junior summer offer from Centerview last night.”

    “Fuck yeah. You’re set. Return offer’s like, guaranteed for those, right?”

    “Something like that.”

    Before we realized, James was marching straight at us across Massachusetts Ave. He looked like some kind of miniature superhero, as he had the general shape and musculature of a low-level bodybuilder (off-season, of course), just scaled down to his height. He was doing his stupid ‘crossing-the-street-on-the-longest-diagonal-possible’ act to perform his arrival. It was funny every time though, and only one car stopped and honked at him this instance.

    “We ready to go get some boba?” James did like a double hi-five thing with Lily. And they started speaking some unintelligible dialect of North American English. That was how they were.

    “We’ve been ready James,” Caden said. “And Lord knows you do not need anymore of that shit.”

    “And does that mean you’re not excited to meet my amazing friend Grace, as well as eat some delectable Sichuan cuisine?” I asked.

    “Lah-si-gee,” James blabbered. He was trying to say làzijī, the only dish name from that restaurant he could remember. He’s been doing that since we started planning this trip.

—

    “Wait, so where’s Centerview again?” Caden asked. Lily and James were on the other side of the 1 bus, effortlessly entertained with each other’s presences. Giggleheads. I could make out a ‘lah-si-gee’ amidst their quibbling.

    “It’s on 52nd, straight Midtown,” I replied.

    “Ok, so it’s in New York.”

    “Obviously it’s in New York.”

    “You know I’m not too great with your banking stuff.”

    “Alright, man.”

    Caden rolled his shoulders back. He was wearing a dark gray hoodie today. “But that’s really great. So you’re like, set set? Like you’re good for postgrad, basically?”

    “I think so. From what I understand, the hard part’s behind me. Me getting this junior summer offer was the big thing. I can finally take a load off.”

    The buses in Boston were brutally slow. The T too. BU and Newbury were so close geographically to Harvard but there’s no T line going straight across the Charles here. Today was a sunny day. One of the first of the year. It certainly wasn’t spring here. I used to associate March with the color green because of St. Patrick’s Day and because the little calendars they had at my elementary school always decided to accent the month March with green. I learned last year that it doesn’t become green here until perhaps May. All it does is stop snowing. The sun felt nice through the window. It shined on Caden and I and was warm while we were in the bus. It was still cold outside. Cold sun is a strange thing. I was wearing a hoodie and a black jacket. And some loose jeans: they billowed as I walked and draped over my legs as I sat.

    “And I’m still trying to find out what I’m going to do this summer,” Caden said.

    “You’ve still got time.”

    “Yeah, but I guess it’d be nice to be set like you.”

    “I guess. But all I did was study this horseshit and network and prepare for maybe the last year and a half. I don’t think I’d wish a 5-credit class’s worth of studying on the side for anybody. Definitely not for you.” I sniffed. Caden rested his chin on his thumb as if he had a plural number of thoughts in his head. “And it really is not too interesting. Looking back, violin and those festivals, recitals, competitions, even with all the pressure, were more fun. So was basketball. I just did the necessary work to get the job, to get in here.”

    “You talk low of Centerview and our school—like we don’t go to fucking Harvard.”

    “It’s just fucking Harvard. Still three letters away from being ‘hard.’” I shifted in my seat. Caden didn’t. I had let myself slip a bit. I truly thought there’d be more at this ‘cradle for our nation’s best and brightest,’ but all we did was just go to class during the week and drink when the week ended. No one else really felt that way. They all had that big fish small pond experience upon getting here, being instantly humbled by some ‘true genius’ so they could content themselves with such simple, social pleasures. Except the ‘true genius’ was me, and so, I was left with no one to put me in my place, in any place. I didn’t like talking about my abilities. It made me feel like a narcissist.

    “I think you are simply not a mortal like the rest of us. I find the people here more than competent.”

    “I guess…but I’m not even that smart—it’s not really that hard. And plus, Grace, who you’re about to meet, is probably smarter than I a—”

    “Renee, I think we’re up,” Caden said. The PA system and scrolling text repeated what he said in their own ways: ‘MASS AVE @ NEWBURY.’ I glanced over to James and Lily. Still oblivious. I slapped James on the shoulder and gave Lily a darting look. The four of us stepped off the bus among a blur of young Cambridge professionals, teenagers, and probably other Harvard students on a quest similar to ours.


    Newbury before us. The long line of brownstones cast itself expansively in front. This river of people before me, the apparition of these faces. The crowd and the breeze refreshed me in a way. It was always nice to get out of Cambridge. Even just across the water was enough.

    “So where’s your friend?” Lily asked, zipping her white puffer to the top to adjust to the wind. She had a pink stole that characterized her appearance in the cold months. People become identifiable that way during winter and its derivatives. A particular coat and a particular accessory and probably some jeans below. You really have a sense that you know who exactly they are. They’re the gov major from Chapel Hill who’s half-Chinese half-Slovenian, the diligent and friendly and open and overall just rather pleasant girl who I lived with who always wore a white puffer and a pink stole during winter. I don’t know what the hell I am.

    “She said she was walking here,” I said. “She might be a tad late.”

    “From BU? In this weather? That’s gotta be like at least fifteen minutes. And only if she’s closer to Fenway.”

    “I remember that she talked about going on walks in high school. She’s probably strolling along, taking her time.” Grace walked very slowly, so I knew that that fifteen minute walk was probably at least twenty-two for her. She walked with a straight but fluid motion, like a model but without that sense that she’s on a runway or stage. She almost walked perfectly, if there was such a thing.

    …

    “Have y’all been waiting long?” Grace Huang had arrived. She had stuck her head over my right shoulder to make her inquiry. She embodied a moray eel.

    “No, not at all. It’s good to see you,” I said.

    “Likewise. It’s been some time.” Grace circled her gaze round our little troupe. “So…what are y’all’s names?”

    “Lily, Lily Kovac. Nice to meet you.”

    “My name’s Caden.”

    “James Le!”

    It felt strange with Grace here. Like I was creating some kind of unholy fusion between people from different worlds. The composite was stranger than the sum of its parts. Even now, Grace had that peculiar aura that seemed to distinguish her from all else. Made us seem lowly. Or maybe noble.

—

    James and Lily had accumulated a considerable arrangement of shopping bags. James also had a colorful drink in his right hand. Orange and white. Creamsicle-esque. They were some four heads ahead of us in the crowd. I hadn’t felt like buying anything, neither did Grace, and whether Caden bought anything or not we couldn’t tell, as he could stuff whatever into his massive pants pockets. The sun shined and the wind sped in long, invisible lines down Newbury. My hair whipped behind me. The air felt nice on my neck.

    “—think I might just go back home to New York, work somewhere in town for the summer,” Grace said. “I think I’ll self-study some more linear algebra, like what you’d see in a second class in it, to prepare myself for future physics classes. But I mostly want to spend a lot of time painting. I have a lot of visions that I need to see through. I want to experiment a lot with texture to create this parti—” Me, Grace, and Caden were left lagging in the back. The conversation inevitably drifted towards the summer and future once more. It seemed that was the direction we all were headed.

    “And so how long have you been painting?” Caden asked. The wind had changed orientations, and Caden was covertly, nonchalantly, struggling to keep his fringe out of his eyes. It was cute.

    “I started doing it in my own time during high school, but I always found it a lot of fun in art class growing up. It’s one of the few things in this world that I can really get lost in, for like hours, and it’s become real entangled with my life; whether I’m painting or drawing more or less has become rather indicative of my mid-term mental/emotional state.” Grace was awkwardly twisted around at us as she spoke. She was walking ahead of us, and her neck was turned quite far, owl-like. As she explained, she looked at us intently, with curiosity, with a pale glow in her iris. “Do you know what I mean? Like, Caden, what do you do for fun?”

    “For fun…hmmm…I guess I go to the gym every other day or so…but I mostly just go to class, do homework, and hang out with my friends. I used to play violin and soccer for a lot of the time before going here.”

    “Did Renee mention that she also plays vio—”

    “Played. Too much IB, banking nonsense.” I smiled half-convincingly.

    “Ah, I guess you haven’t kept up…” She turned back to Caden: “But she played violin. I went to one of her recitals in senior year and I still remember that performance to this day. She had a lovely tone. Played with such passion. Evoked it with the instrument. I think she was playing the Mendelssohn, was it? She was our school’s concertmaster too.” Grace’s hair wasn’t as long as mine. It made her head seem light. Instead of flowing long strands it was more like a singular shape centered at her head, like an upside-down tulip. Or a lampshade.

    “I think she mentioned that she used to play before, but that’s cool to hear. It seems that she’s a prodigy in all regards, no?” Caden asked.

    “She was also captain of our basketball team. Made it to states senior year.”

    “No way. Yet one more addition to the list of ‘things Renee is better than me at.’”

    I couldn’t take all the talk about me. “I’m really not as great as all that. I’d just been doing all those things since I was tiny. There were always better players, of violin, of basketball. I was only second row 1st violin in All-State, and we got knocked out pretty quickly at that state tournament.”

    “Second row 1st in New York All-State means you were pretty fucking good, no? Like you could’ve gone to a decent music school, right? I think Mass’s All-State is comparable, if not worse, and I barely scraped in and only did so in senior year. But I knew some people from there, not just the principals, who went to pretty solid music programs, just sub-Julliard,” Caden said.

    “I guess I could have. I never really gave music school too much thought. Violin probably helped me get in here though. I think I wrote one of my supplements about it,” I said.

    James and Lily had gotten far ahead of us. They made it across a crosswalk right before the light turned red. Me, Grace, and Caden suddenly stopped, joined the growing amalgamation of people at the curb.

    “Renee, you were brilliant.” Grace stared at me with the resolve of an aged general. The three of us stood on the corner like statues, or redwoods.

    “I guess,” I said. “I was kind of burnt out after high school.”

    “I mean, it’s never too late to get back into it. Recently, I’ve been feeling like I want to paint for the rest of my life. It’s getting more fun as I get better. I can do more, portray truer to what I conceive. Even if you picked up violin again two years after we graduate, you’ll still be ahead of my painting, years-wise.”

    “I guess.”

    …

    “So, does everyone from Northern Westchester or whatever have insane artistic talent or something?” Caden felt the need to add to the conversation.

    “Maybe,” I said. The walk signal came on. Lily and James were staring at us from across the street, standing stock still, trying to look like mannequins. The flow of people was unleashed. Shoulders and backpacks and tendon-ed hands drifted forth. I was one of the taller ones in the mass; I could somewhat see this ocean of tops of heads and hair, much of the hair sleek and onyx and whip-like, like mine. I thought about what Grace said. I looked around. I wondered if that’s what I’d become one day: another working professional, perhaps someone important like a VP or Partner at one of the banks. And I’d be known for my strong organizational skills, my vision—my sheer competence and diligence from my time as an Analyst; for my having gone to Harvard, more so for my MBA than my undergrad (that was for granted); maybe even for my height, my dusty, focused eyes, or the way the long black drapes and curtains coming off my scalp danced around and caressed my cheekbones—and perhaps then, and only then, would we get to my once having played violin, my ‘second row 1st violin at All-State’ that will have grown ever small and distant by then, like a star out of my window in Cambridge, or a speck of dust in lamplight, and I’d ask myself, as I ask myself now, ‘Was it even real? That I once dedicated my life to this ornate wooden box with metal wires? To an orange ball that bounces?’ and then I’d take another sip of my drink, vodka, plain, at the firm’s happy hour, at a friend’s, or on my lonesome in a quiet studio apartment kitchen, and then I’d recall the offering memorandum we needed to finish by Tuesday.

    “I think I can see Boston Common,” James said.

    Caden jabbed James’s side. “That’s the Public Garden, bud.”

—

    “You know, I’ve been in Boston for all but two years now, and this might be the second time I’ve been to the Public Garden,” Grace said. “It’s really nice. The water’s pretty.” It tracked that she didn’t explore too much. She found ways to keep herself busy.

    “I’ve maybe been a few times,” I said. “I try to walk through it every time I take a trip across the water. Usually with these clowns.” I really liked walking on Commonwealth instead of Newbury when I went to the Garden. The statues and trees between all that brick were mystical. Especially in fall.

    “Who’s a clown?” James turned sharply towards me.

    “Glad you knew that was aimed at you, bud.” I flashed him a grin.

    I could begin to see the glass towers of downtown. A small slice of them was revealed between these last brownstones of Newbury. Boston was so much shorter, wider, sparser than New York; in New York the streets felt like channels; the buildings rose forever on your left and right, making each street a long corridor to somewhere, someplace. Here there were just a bunch of various things scattered: synagogues on Beacon, bits of green, sidewalk trees, as well as indistinguishable homes and businesses. The lack of glass made it all so much less transparent (obviously). But something about that non-opacity of New York made me think of it as a lonely place, that amidst all the noise and bustle, you were really, truly alone. I couldn’t imagine being with my friends there and having our existences define the sidewalk, having our aura and glow carve out a space out of that gray land. James, Lily, Caden, Grace and I shone with something invisible, that made it so among these walking, talking Bostonians, we were ourselves, we were friends, we were out. And I felt kind of lonely last night too, getting the offer. No one was there to congratulate me. It was nighttime in my dormitory. I felt a little like that when I first got into Harvard, after the excitement died down, after the tight hugs with my parents and frantic FaceTimes; I had laid on my bed supine and stared at the ceiling, because while I was about to go somewhere new, brilliant, life-changing, it all too was coming to an end, my entire life at the time, and it seems that this one (life) too is ending right as it’s getting started: Caden, Lily, James at some point soon would just become my ‘college friends,’ Grace even more distant. It all seemed to be a slow, lonely march to the top of a hill that wasn’t particularly high. I like banking; I like Harvard; but there was to be, ought to be, something else, something more. Everything seems to be set.

    …

    But we had crossed into the park, and I could see the pond and the willows. They looked like they came from another world. Exhibited some kind of feathered beauty.

    “Lah-si-gee,” James chirped.

    “Yeah, I’m kind of getting hungry too,” I replied. Grace and Lily were just ahead talking big about global affairs. Those things tired me. It was good that they were getting along already. Caden was staring off to the right, at a crowd or bird or magnolia. Perhaps he had run out of things to say.

    “So, I know now what you’re doing next summer, but how about this one?” James’s fingers were interlaced. I was growing tired of the subject. I wanted to get to the restaurant soon.

    “I’m working at a smaller boutique bank at Stamford. I’ll probably be commuting from home.”

    “Banking, banking, banking. I shouldn't have asked. Are you going to be doing anything fun this summer?”

    “Banking is fun…” I said this quarter-convincingly. “I was talking with Grace and Caden about this earlier, but I might try to pick up violin again. I haven’t really played since before I got here—I mean, to school.”

    …

    “I’m going to be at Merkenschlager’s lab this summer. I finally got funding secured.” James had arrived to the point of this conversation.

    “That’s awesome! What are you going to be doing there? Something cellular?”

    “Yeah. Something something vaccines, something germinal center, something T follicular helper cell, blah blah blah. Immune system nonsense. The gist is is that her lab studies a particular part of the immune response, which could help with developing better vaccines.”

    “Nice. Real noble stuff. I feel like J.P. Morgan or something, some 21st century robber-baron. Look at you, saving lives and whatnot.”

    “Nah, I’m no lifesaver. I just really like B cells. I bet Merkenschlager’s like that too, just with the TFH cells. All the vaccine talk’s probably just to keep the lights on at the lab.”

    We had reached the bridge. Over the water, to my left and right, the view opened up. I could see all the willows, hanging, suspended, each wispy line of green stuck in perpetual frozen plummet, and I could see those Boston brick buildings, solid with glass eyes.

    “I’m so excited for the lah-si-gee.”

    “Là, zi, jī,” I enunciated, pronouncing each character with the perfect, slow pronunciation they would drill into us at Chinese school. “And I hope you can try something else this time. Maybe we can get you to eat some niúwā: bullfrog.”

    “God forbid a Viet boy speak a little Chinese. And eating frog might be a little out there for me.”

    “I think they eat bullfrogs in Vietnam too, in like a stew or soup or something.”

    “Ok, well I’m not that Viet. No bullfrog entering this temple of a body.”

    “But that second boba did.”

    James held both his hands in the air. “What can I say?”


    We were entering Boston Common now, and the trimmed hedges, flowering shrubs, and snaking trees gave way to the sprawling, soft hills and footpaths of the park. It seems that the Public Garden is the only place where spring comes on time. Some unholy rites or sacrifices likely involved.

    “Are you and Lily living together again next year?” James asked.

    “Yeah, neither of us are transferring out of Dunster and I can’t really think of anyone else who I’d like to room with there. It’s been great living with her. She beats my first-year roommates by a longshot.”

    “Yeah, isn’t Lily the best?” James said. It seemed that Grace and Lily were getting real passionate about whatever overlooked war they were squabbling about. Grace’s hair was wobbling aggressively, as was Lily’s pink stole. Caden was now deciding which conversation to tune into. “I’m also not switching houses, but I’m going to gun for a single this time. My roommate’s a fucking slob. He’s always on reels or playing games on his PC.”

    The vast expanse of Boston Common opened it all up, again reminded me of Boston’s comparative width. We were at the foot of Downtown. Brick changed into glass. The city’s sole skyscrapers were all here; all around us. And the trimmed grass and white dudes dressed in shorts for no reason and mild sky up above just seemed to stretch on and on. Strange that we were in the middle of a city. The dull grass still somehow had so much life in it. Teeming. Lily and Grace slowed a bit. We could begin to hear their conversation.

    “—but are we not to provide direct support to the Sahel States, especially as Wagner’s pulled out?” Grace said. “Is that not how we build coalitions and more importantly, prevent unnecessary tragedy?” So it was the AES they were talking about.

    “—policing the world anymore. The obvious examples: Iraq, Afghanistan, soon-to-be Venezuela, and in Africa: Congo-Kinshasa 1961, Mogadishu, fucking Egypt and Project FF,” Lily said. “It all never changes anything and usually just makes things worse. And it’s especially tiring given that all that money is being withheld from—” Her stole was flapping with a ferocity I thought not possible. “—would intervening in Mali be any different? And plus, there’s nothing in it for this administration. There’s always an ulterior, material motive—”

    I knew a little bit about the whole ordeal because Lily’s ranted to me about it every now and then, especially now that it has supplanted Sudan as the world’s currently most overlooked conflict. I’ve heard variations of this spiel and I’ve grown too tired to engage her in discussion. I lived with her, after all. I’m glad that she’s met her match in Grace.

    “—I could foresee a future of growing African internal infrastructure, for that’s one of the key things the region, especially Sub-Saharan, truly lacks, needs—and in that future, perhaps the Sahel would fulfill its once historical place: of consolidating power via facilitating connection, that Saharan corridor,” Grace said. “Investing in that prospect, as an ally, or partner, wouldn’t be a bad move. But I’m reaching too far; even if I’m right, no government—”

    Politically, historically, those two were on another level. It made sense for Lily because she was studying the stuff and she planned on working in international law in the future, but Grace just seemed to have picked it up from curious wanderings. I don’t even know how she has time to study Sub-Saharan African history and politics. She was a fucking physics major. Physics and visual arts. It was absurd.

    “—ought to decide for themselves. I believe in Traoré and the AES,” Lily said. “They might be lacking in firepower and resources right now, but I think the cutting of all these ties, with France, Russia, will serve them well in the next ten—”

    I really didn’t get it. How they could talk so passionately about lands they’ve never set eyes upon, about people they’ve never met. Our lives are barely together in this supposed ‘educational center of the U.S.,’ and somehow we’re able to extend our concern to the globe? It was noble in a way, but it just didn’t make sense. All my time was taken up by homework, studying banking, and spending time with my friends. Sure, I used my phone a bit too much, but they certainly did too, Lily definitely—well, perhaps not Grace. But how did they do it? Those great world-changing men and women through history? Did Martin Luther King Jr. not worry about his career? Where he would live?

    “—longshot though. The disparity of wealth between postcolonial states and the outside world is too great. Unless they completely shut themselves out from the world, in practice too, the value of foreign currency would prove too strong, and the corruption that’s plagued the continent will forever perpetuate, upheld by aid agencies, ports, and patro—”

    Even then, that wasn’t really what I wanted. I don’t know what I want. Even for something smaller like Grace’s painting, how does she convince herself that it’ll all be okay? Just studying physics and art like that’ll get her a job? The pennies she’d make as a grad student would be monumental compared to whatever she’d make trying to pursue art. She was so naive, so blind to it all. She didn’t know how hard a painting hobby is to keep without the funds to afford the supplies necessary. And to sustain that artist’s mind, one needs to be fresh. What if she was so extinguished by whatever she did for work that instead of getting home and painting each day, she would instead retire to her couch, let fatigue take over? And then five years would go by and her ‘visions she needed to see through’ would remain just visions, immaterial. Dreams of being an artist. Of being a violinist. It’ll all get flushed down the drain when she graduates, when we graduate. And it’s the same for me. I’ll stop thinking about these things then, stop considering my life with respect to possibilities, choices, and futures, and slowly, like erosion, begin to see it as a static thing, as a routine, as a single state: that of being a banker—an Analyst, Associate, VP, MD, Partner—and that’s what I, Renee Xu, would be, for many long years and decades until I retired. Then I wouldn’t even be any of that anymore. I don’t know what I’d be then. A bag of once-was’s, black hair (not even), and money.

    “We’re supposed to go this way, right?” James pointed down the path to our right. He was correct; we were supposed to exit Boston Common on the Southeast corner to get spat out into Chinatown.

    “Yeah. That way to Chinatown. To Làzijī,” I answered.

    “Lah-si-gee.”

—

    The restaurant was crowded. It was dark. Dry wood lined everything. All lights were soft yellow. There were some paper lamps. A waiter approached us.

    “Jǐweì?” he asked.

    “Wŭweì,” I replied.

    “Zhèbiānr.” He grabbed some menus and began to walk.

    I gestured to the group to follow the waiter. He was clean, had a sleek face, as well as a trendy textured hairstyle like the mainland celebrities. Probably an international student somewhere around here. My Chinese wasn’t bad. I could survive interactions like those. At this place, if you look East Asian they’ll start speaking to you in Chinese. It’s nice when people assume things about you and they’re right. I took any chance to practice my speaking, as I only did so on the phone with my mom.

    “Lah-si-gee.” I had come here before with Lily and James, before they introduced me to Caden (though we had met before, before we were friends). My parents weren’t from the South, but we’ve always had a taste for Sichuan food. We had this place we liked in the city, in Flushing that we’d go to every now and then. I missed that restaurant. I missed my parents. When I first started college I was so eager to get out of my house: no more yelling about school, practices, practicing; no more of the constant interrogations about what I was doing, what I was to do, etc. They had a suffocating presence, as well as a firm belief that everyone ought to burn themselves at both ends like they had done for most of their lives. But they were always there. And they cut me some fruit every night. I missed all that.

    “Alright, so we’re getting làzijī, some rice. I’ll cast a vote for this cold chicken dish,” Lily said. Her mom was actually from Sichuan province, and while she couldn’t do anything more Chinese-speaking-wise but pronounce characters correctly, she knew what dishes were good from the pictures here. We had found this place together.

    “I’m down,” I said. “I also think we should get suāncàiyú, sour cabbage fish soup. It sounds a little weird but it’s very good. That’ll be my one dish I’ll enforce upon y’all today.” I pushed my menu to the table’s center.

    “Oh, wait, Lily, what was that eel dish we had last time?” James was about to violate his one-dish suggestion limit that I had at some point designated in my mind.

    “The dry chili eel. We can get that too,” she replied.

    Grace contemplated the menu. We could probably afford (stomach-budget) one more dish; we weren’t too hungry. “This pork kidney stir-fry looks good,” she said. She then looked at Caden. “Anything you want to throw in the order?”

    “Nah, I trust you guys. I’ll eat whatever.” That was very Caden. He masked his fatal indecisiveness with a demeanor of cool. That constant guile that only I and perhaps Grace could see through made him fallible in a way, vulnerable because he was vulnerable without knowing it—I doubt his mask was one worn intentionally, likely habit—and I found that charming in a way, his falsity, and his unawareness of the holes in it. Because he was a rather pleasing and affable sort of fellow underneath. I’ve found that I can read most people. You can tell a lot from the way one carries themselves, the rhythm of their speech, and their walking pace. But I wonder if people could do the same for me. See. See more than my grades and face and career plans and once-did activities in high school and summer plans and see the doubt underneath. That I really am as lost as the rest of them. That I might be more lost because there’s no good reason for me to feel so. I got into Harvard. I got all A’s. I got the job. If I came across a person so skilled in deception that I couldn’t read them, I’d think they were a monster. It’s monstrous to be able to do that: to be able to create another person that’s so full, round, complete, and convincing that you can completely hide beneath it, go completely undetected. I fear that I’m a little like that. Inscrutable.

    “So, Grace, how have you found us so far?” James asked. We were at the final destination of our quest, of our day in Boston. Our time with her would close soon. The paper lamps stared. The staff had brought us our waters.

    “You guys are great. I mean, y’all are friends with Renee so I wouldn’t’ve expected less. I’m glad to see she’s got good people around here at the big H.” She always called Harvard that ever since I got in—no, ever since I applied.

    Lily was now stole-less. We were in a six-person booth with the sixth seat being reserved for various bags and jackets and the pink stole. The booth felt like a small enclosure, dry wood. Comforting though. “And it’s been great to meet you, Grace,” Lily said. “Renee’s never really talked much about her home and high school, besides that it’s in ‘Northern Westchester.’ It’s been nice to get a glimpse into what sort of life she was leading before we knew her.”

    “To be fair, I’m not sure Renee and I were the most representative sample of our high school; we were kind of odd in some way,” Grace said.

    “Speak for yourself.” I smiled.

    “Well, at least I was. But I’m glad that this has been such a fun day. I don’t really get out too much. Maybe just to Fens sometimes. Walking around Boston has been refreshing. Meeting y’all has.”

    I took a sip from my glass. It was perhaps beautiful. We existed in our own space, in this booth. The glow was here. We were sitting in a wooden booth at a Sichuan restaurant, about to enjoy a splendid meal.

    “Yeah, it’s been great,” piped James.

    We all paused for a second. Caden stared at his glass. James tapped the underside of the table. Grace was staring off somewhere. Having Grace here, it made everything so different. I’d been hanging out with these folks for the better part of a year and a half and somehow the addition of her presence made it all change. We felt nobler, fresher, purer somehow. There was a certain comfort, comfort that yet existed in this silence. It was the silent knowledge of our entire lives, between me and Grace, that we did not speak about, rarely spoke about, as we never needed to. I knew everything about Caden, James, and Lily. Their high school cultures, relationships with parents, love lives (especially Caden), niche opinions, living habits (mainly Lily), and American regional perspectives. We had talked about it all so much in our time together, living on the same campus. I realized that I never really talked about these things with Grace; they went unspoken, presupposed. We just knew each other in that way. We could assume that about each other, and be right.

    “Our food’s here.” Grace’s staring in the distance paid off. We all turned to see the array of dishes coming our way.

    “That looks so fucking good.” Caden’s eyes were transfixed.

    “Lah-si-gee.”

    Ornate blue and white china. Fried meats covered in piles of dried chilies. Steam and spice radiated from each plate. Five small bowls of rice. Pieces of chicken in a crimson-fire sauce. Delectable, splendid. I could make out a sparkle in James’s eyes. And a final waiter came, holding a massive, lidded bowl. Heat emanated from a small hole somewhere on the lid as he carried it. He held it with care and purpose. My sour fish soup. Our sour fish soup.

    It all looked brilliant on our table. James, Lily, and Caden took out their phones and snapped pictures of the feast. The steam fogged up their cameras. I felt hungrier than I did prior. Grace was smiling. It was ever brighter: the glow—the glow that made our existences justified, distinguishable from the crowd and the noise. We were here. It seemed that we had collectively obtained some of that air of clarity Grace always carried. It surrounded us. Enveloped us. Sour heat and numbing spice spread through it all. We were beautiful. We were eager. We had walked down Newbury, and through the green, to make it here. The blue-white china stood out on the dead brown table. Brought brimming contrast and lightness to the surface. It all looked brilliant.

    “Shall we?” Lily asked as she unfolded her napkin on her lap.

    “We shall.” James had already found the làzijī and began to shovel it onto his plate.

    I looked at Caden. He looked at me. We smiled. I looked at Grace. She seemed happy.

—

    There was still a little light out. At least we had left winter in that regard. Boston’s Chinatown was a daytime place. A place to eat proper meals, and to go grocery shopping. It looked a little eerie as it turned night, as the only signs that were lit up were for establishments that were markedly non-Chinese: Bank of America, CVS, Dunkin, as well as the logo for the T. When it got dark Chinatown just became another part of Boston. All the uncles with their hands clasped behind their back and aunties pushing wheeled carts were asleep. The day was ending.

    “So, what are you going to get up to after this, Centerview girl?” It was just me and Grace. The other three were off entertaining some wild craving of James, probably boba. They maybe figured I wanted some time to chat with just Grace, just the two of us.

    “I think I might go over to Caden’s. I think Lily’s going too. James has got some absurd lab report he needs to finish tonight so he won’t be joining us. We’re probably going to drink a bit and then go out.”

    “A proper end to this day of celebration. You deserve nothing less.” She beamed a bit.

    “Yeah.”

    The moon was already out. It was less than halfway. I had the impression that it was staring at me, and that the stars in the sky were a bunch of little eyes, watching me, Renee Xu.

    …

    Grace was staring South down Washington. With her head turned, I could imagine that I never knew her, that she was another passing face in Boston, or Harvard. She looked wise. It was part of her air. She was just as compelling as she looked. I was glad to be friends with her, to have known her for so long. We’ll probably be friends forever—till we die. But we were walking different paths. Her, with her physics and art. Me, with banks in New York. Where was she going to go? Off to Amsterdam? A PhD program in the Midwest? China? Meanwhile I will have stayed essentially where I’ve always been, where we’ve always been: New York, tristate, ‘Northern Westchester’—I mean, I’d probably travel around in the summers, but all the banks were there, the city, and starting now, I’d probably be forever anchored to that place. I already feel that pull, pulling me away from here, Harvard, Boston. But I’ve been tethered much longer than that—since I sent out my first application; since I joined HFA; since I first started studying the basics: accounting and whatnot; and since I had that inkling of an idea during, what was it? senior year that I’d be an investment banker, that that’d be a good use of my talents, abilities: aptitude in math, capacity to work, leadership, communication, all that horseshit. None of it was hard. Communication just meant keeping everyone including yourself posted. Leadership was just doing the necessary work to get the big things done. But was that it? New York? Centerview? It seemed that Grace was still free, strolling around in a field somewhere, slowly, feeling the grass, the breeze, wearing a white dress, letting the golden sun shower her in ever-beauty. And I wanted to call out to that apparition of her. Say something. Ask her, “Will you let me walk with you?” My eyes were wet. Grace turned around.

    “Grace, do you miss home?”

    “Yeah.” She took a long breath, looked for a second somewhere up above. “Especially this last winter. I feel like the excitement of moving out’s worn off and we’re left tired with no particular source of comfort, stability.” Grace didn’t mince words. I could make out the first twinkle of a star. Night was beginning to fall.

    “I’ve loved almost every second of college,” I said. “But it’s those times in between, times like now, like when I was sitting by myself with that job offer last night, that a real darkness seeps in.” Grace looked at me intently. “It’s like all we’ve been left with is just a vague sense of insecurity.”

    “I feel that,” she replied.

    A stream of wind hit us. Blew my hair into my face. I flipped my head and held up my hand to keep the strands out of my face. Grace’s danced elegantly. She turned towards me.

    “Renee, if it’s any consolation—”

    I was crying. The sky looked like glass, half-night, and the moon was beginning to light up. The light in the sky you could set your eyes upon. There were many colors in the sky I couldn’t name. The Bank of America sign was red, white, and blue. Grace’s hair was black. Droplets on a Boston sidewalk.

    “I want to play violin.”

—

    The four of us were sitting on the red line on our way back to Harvard. I figured my eyes were red too. I was still thinking about what Grace said, her consolation. She was gone now. We were at the day’s inflection point on the T: the border of the last commuters and the earliest nightwalkers. There were two extremes on this car tonight: eternal fatigue and newborn nocturnal excitement. The four of us were somewhere in between. James’s eyes were closed. Lily was staring at her reflection in the window. Caden was next to me, glancing around the car, his eyes settling on me every now and then.

    I turned my head. “When should I be at yours tonight?”

    “I think I want to shower first and then clean up the place a bit, maybe like 9:30,” he replied.

    “Alright, that works. Lily and I’ll be there then.”

    I looked up. Red accents. Orange digi-text signage. We left Grace at Downtown Crossing. She was going to walk to the green line to go back to BU. She was probably on it now. I wondered if her car was above ground yet. Green line at night. Slow trundle through Boston’s dark blanket.

    “Today was fun.” Caden couldn’t take the silence. Me neither.

    “Yes. Seeing Grace, being in Boston, right after getting that offer: it’s made me real reflective.”

    “You do seem wiser than usual.”

    “I’m always wise.”

    “Then right now you are exceptionally wise.”

    “Thanks.” I sniffed.

    “I noticed you were…um…were you—are you doing alright?”

    “No, yeah. I just got a little teared up when I was speaking with Grace while we were waiting for y’all. It’s what I said just now. This day, the offer. It’s made me think a bunch about past, present, and future. Perhaps a bit too much.” I took a breath. “And I was scared, scared that I’d just walk a lonely, narrow path as my life went on, after graduating from here. The feeling’s passed. I’m not sure I’ve come to any particular resolution.”

    “Damn. I’m sorry.”

    “Don’t be.”

    Caden took a breath. He was staring straight ahead, out the window. Train rails grinding. “We just gotta keep up with each other. You kept up with Grace. You’ll keep keeping up with her. You better keep up with us. There’s not a whole lot more we can do, but that’s all we got to do.”

    “But why? We just keep drifting apart. It won’t be the same.”

    “It won’t.”

    The train started to decelerate. ‘HARVARD SQUARE.’

—

    It felt like last night. Lily went off with James, so I had walked home alone. I was back in here, on the couch. The lights from Harvard Square were once again peering through the glass’s crystal. The lamp’s light once again captured a cone of fluttering dust. It had been a long day. We had done so much. But nothing in particular had changed. I wished the world would change.

    I flipped myself in a strange way so that I could let my head hang off the couch like I had it hang off my bed earlier today. Even with the lights on, LEDs buzzing, the small squares of night through the windows seemed to infect the room. It was bright but it felt dark. The ceiling lights cast three weak shadows off of the lamp’s base. Long figures going in all different directions. I felt the blood pool in my head; I could never hold myself in this position for very long, but I felt like testing myself this time. Tension built. I think my hair was touching the floor. We forgot to vacuum this week. I wondered if Grace had gotten back yet. Maybe she was strolling around, staring at the stars; they were probably quite visible now. She would be walking with that poise, smoothness, and she would have that air, that space that carved itself out of the night, out of Boston. Her eyebrows would be relaxed, unfurled, well above her eyes. Her gaze would be open, and she would look upon each stranger that passed, and she would wonder as to what they were doing, what they did, what they were like—and, just as quickly, she would turn her attention back to a passing building, the sky above again, or a pothole, or a door. Or maybe she was sitting on her couch, or bed, just like me. Maybe she too was letting her head hang off the edge, letting all that pressure seep into every orifice of her scalp and skull. And her hair would just flop over—too short to hang suspended like the willows—and she would stare at her wall, her roommate’s bedframe, her laundry basket. Maybe she was crying. I wondered how she was doing.

    I moved to my bed, curled up. I was so tired. The lights were off. My phone glowed white. I was looking at my own Instagram page. I looked so happy, hanging out with my friends, walking the stage at graduation, swimming at the beach. It all felt so far away right now. The only thing real was the dusk in my bedroom.

    I went to Caden’s page. He looked cool and attractive and competent. He didn’t have a lot of posts. They were mostly just him in a row with other dudes in the same exact pose with the same exact expression. Whoever was on the end of each row would always put up a little sideways peace sign, like ‘scissors’ in rock paper scissors. It was a little pathetic. I then went to the page of this girl who was in one of my stats classes. She had a blog linked to her page. It was full of poetry, short pieces about movies she’s watched, and little journal-entry-like posts. None of it was particularly good. I only read the poetry. How did she put it all out there? It was barely okay, yet she still felt compelled to write and post all of it. She’d been posting since she got to college. Did she not read her own work? Realize that she was just putting out far too personal mediocrity? But part of me was jealous. Because she did, and because she perhaps did not know. Or maybe she knew she wasn’t that good, but she kept writing and posting anyway. It was noble and pathetic in a way. I was jealous of that.

    …

    I put my phone somewhere on the bed near me. I turned onto my back. The dots on the ceiling formed waves. The darkness in my room was opaque. The darkness out the window was clear. Stars.

    …

    Yesterday I had received my offer for a junior summer internship at Centerview. I had spent the last year and a half studying finance on my own time for this. I had spent the last two semesters networking with every banker under the sun. I had spent the last two months interviewing at every firm under the New York sun. I had spent the last three weeks preparing extra hard, doing mock interviews with HFA, studying and practicing every single thing I missed, for my Super Day for Centerview. Centerview was all I wanted. I answered to the best of my ability, as clearly, intelligently, and impressively as I could, every single question thrown at me during those four thirty-minute interviews that it seemed my entire life hinged upon. Yesterday I had received my offer for a junior summer internship at Centerview. They had above a 95% return rate. I was set for life.

—

    “Renee, Lily, long time no see,” Caden said.

    “Same for yourself,” I replied.

    There weren’t many people there yet. But Caden’s room was small, and there were perhaps ten faces I knew and maybe three friends of Caden’s I didn’t know—all congregated around a table. The place was full enough. New faces were always interesting. There were purple lights all over the walls and ceiling.

    Lily pointed at the handle of vodka, “Shall we begin?” The music was loud.

    “We shall.” Caden poured three shots in those shitty plastic mini-red-solo-cups. Lily grabbed hers, and Caden, with that sirenic glimmer in his eye, held one cup for himself and extended another to me, and there was that air of beauty that shone iridescently and rosy between us—I grabbed the cup and raised it. His eyes flickered in the darkness. The three of us touched our cups together.

    “To Renee, and this splendid day in Boston, and her amazing Centerview offer,” Caden toasted.

    Yes, he was right. The day was splendid. I ought to celebrate.
