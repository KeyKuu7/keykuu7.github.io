Running Away: On Writing Moral Scrupulosity

Back to home 31 Mar 2026

Originally published in the Spring 2026 edition of the Bowdoin Review

    I’ve begun reading Rabbit, Run by John Updike, and I struggle. I sit down and open the book and always seem to find that I soon after put it down. I’ve found myself reading online articles and forum posts about the book more than the book itself, trying to understand my inability to get lost in the prose. I scroll through online folks’ praises, revilements—articles’ explanations, histories, searching for a reason: a reason to press through the text; a reason to put it back on the shelf; a reason to take any action to make that distracting feeling go away.

    I read online that Updike is one of the great American postwar novelists…that his prose and style are brilliant…perhaps his substance not so much…that he’s misogynistic…that we are supposed to hate Rabbit…or he was too sympathetic to Rabbit (hence misogyny)...he was satirizing Kerouac’s On the Road…that Rabbit’s adultery is compelling because it hits so close to home…for who?...but he was really just a gifted stylist obsessed with sex and midcentury WASP culture…but that was just a vehicle to touch on universal themes…universal themes of masculinity…and adultery…and…

    None of these are satisfying. They rather feel deficient.


    Rabbit is a piece of shit. This is obvious. Updike intended this. If the book is in part a criticism of On the Road, then Rabbit is an attack on the ignorant neglect and moral vapidity that constitute a good part of Sal Paradise and Dean Moriarity. But part of the untruth I sense in the above impressions is that I feel Updike is far too unsympathetic to Rabbit, that he does not portray deeper layers of the character, lest they humanize him. It feels as if Updike has created a character that he wishes to crucify, and portrays all his shameful deeds exactly as they are. Updike does not press to next layers.

    Rabbit has issues. He doesn’t want to blame himself. He blames his wife. His wife is dumb. These kinds of sequences of thought are littered through the book’s opening, and they are utterly insufficient tries of getting into the head of such a man as Rabbit. The real draw of, the potential in writing, filthy and reprehensible characters is not in their filth, their diagnosis. It’s boring. We all go through our daily lives subconsciously, snidely disparaging all those in every way lesser than us, providing round, full analyses of their personality defects, antisocial tics, and inconsistencies of the heart. To do this to a character in fiction is not revolution. The real draw of writing through pieces of shit is that they force us to confront their sickening conditions as part of our fundamental sameness. That we may identify a part of ourselves in them should first revolt us, then expand us.

    When an author peers through the very soul of those who we can so instinctively, instantly, readily brand as scrupulous, of flinching character, and shows us—reminds us that they are made of the very same bits as you and I, they miraculously, invisibly, draw away our judgment for immersion into the sinner’s heart, such that we may later reckon with the nascent horror from their sense, reason, and sanity. Updike portrays Rabbit’s perspective, but it’s so flat. He’s so fucking boring. I refuse to believe that behind every man who abandons his pregnant wife is solely a planar affect of half-baked boredom. Behind every man is a world unseen to himself. Underneath the skull of the numbest, most placid, flaccid, deadsoul of a human being is a lush interiority. If Rabbit Angstrom is a caricature of Sal Paradise, he does not yet hold that honest yearn, that search for the soul of America that makes him leave behind so many empty-armed. Rabbit Angstrom leaves it all behind to search for nothing in particular. This does not compel me. The bad cannot be taken without the good, and so, when I ask Mr. Updike “Why does Rabbit do this?” he responds plainly, “He wants to go South,” and when I ask him then, “Why does he want that?” Mr. Updike goes silent. Because Rabbit is half of a character, and is not human. Rabbit’s motivations, on a textual level, do not sufficiently justify the uprooting of his life and family. They are just reasons for Rabbit to be fallible, flawed, and critiqueable, reasons for the story to happen.

    It seems that inside Rabbit is bottled up a great many fears, loathings, and flaws that Updike has witnessed or held. I can imagine those uncritical readers, who proclaim this text as revolution, sitting comfortably on their reader’s side of the page, entertaining themselves in their head: either standing gobsmacked by how Updike has brilliantly captured the brutish simplicity of their long-hidden, adulterous urges; or they’re stabbing at Rabbit within the pages of the text with sharpened moral sticks, coolly assured of their own sound character. And it is for the sake of satisfying these rather saccharine, simple readings of the text, for the sake of satisfying some too-righteous readerly/writerly urges on Updike’s part, that Rabbit is damned to stunted humanity. And thus, as he is only half a man, a stunted man, I do not want to see Rabbit redeemed; nor do I want to see him ruined. There is no reason to read about Rabbit Angstrom than to point and laugh. This is not why I read. This is not why I live. Updike does not put me into those deep layers of heart. He does not show me more to Rabbit other than his actions and their first-order self-justifications. For even if a man unconsciously, constantly refuses to interrogate their own decisions and rationale, this must be seen in the text. Every choice explicated and alive. All gods must understand their devils, and to neglect this task is to demonstrate all the ways in which you have been susceptible to them. Updike does not understand, fully, the devil at the helm of his novel, and thus, lays bare to us the readers the exact way in which he constructed the character: Updike has seen something in humanity which upsets him, disgusts him even, and grabs that vile piece and stuffs it into a man in shirt and slacks—or he has seen something in himself, which he loathes, wishes to expunge, and cuts that hunk out of his own flesh, thrusting it into the page and pointing at it, giddily proclaiming “How repulsive!” In either case, he has not constructed a full human being, only a bag of flaws made into fictional agent. And in either case, he has not carved out the whole bit—those parts irredeemable he may see in others are only a product of blindness to the parts yet holy; those parts abhorrent he yet sees in himself had roots, veins, tendrils, that, even after carving out the bit, leave him yet penetrated, susceptible, infected, and compromised. I do not care for writing that is uninterested in the fullness of others nor the depravities of itself. I do not find that compelling. I do not find that human.


    So perhaps this is why I could not lose myself to Rabbit, Run: I demand writing that is morally engaged as well as morally charitable, with both qualities expressed in ways that pound out of the page, expressed to the point of audibility. But alas, this is only my word, and my word is mine alone. For is it truly only because I was wired a certain way and Updike another? Can it be any more than that? I felt some grave injustice between my utter reaction upon reading and the seeming monumentality of this text and its author, and here I am seemingly not set to ease. My criticism is not truth. As is Updike’s. I simply am and think and feel one way or another, and I shall carve that out onto the page, hopefully so rigorously, sharp, and heartful that said carvings may yet justify their own existence. But until then, I put Rabbit, Run back onto the shelf.

    And perhaps I shall pick up Faulkner.
