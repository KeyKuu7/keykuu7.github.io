I. Whispers of greatness

    I feel an intangible, broad malaise. I see taut strings come down from the sky. I have observed the bits of agency that the common questions of my circumstance (what are you doing this summer?) have robbed us of. Something has been lost, corroded, distorted. And I feel the strings tugging on me; their pull subtly steers us towards scripted lives in this world (turn it upside down).

    I reflect on my late years in high school. Those common questions of circumstance that bring me such unease now did the very same then: forcing myself to pick a path to justify my talent, my education, the countless thousands invested in me (not my volition). I began to feel that vague, wavering, forever-extending fear of a dream deferred: that the world is not only indifferent to but hostile to your will; that not only would I, as I was born from dust, return to it, but also in that ephemeral blink of being, instead of doing what I felt was right, being who I felt I was, I would rather agonize over what I did not, in who I was not. In between the cracks of my life then, headphone-laden bus rides, slow walks between classes, and taut sleepless nights, a certain darkness, a void, would drape itself over my reality.

    And down beneath the cracks of my life then, those nights when my mind, instead of closing its eyes—taking its necessary rest—would instead expose them and, within that vast immaterial universe made of not things and being but flows and notions of every color unimaginable, stare at that monochrome galaxy of obsidian grain, that odd spiral disk that seemed like it didn’t belong, that sat apart from all else. That great cloud of black dust would stare back, gaze with a glare that pierced through and past my mind’s eye, that shot into it shaking, revelatory fear. Fear that was soberly clear, real.

    When those nights would come, when the minutes and hours sailed past, when the world outside of my room faded away, and the significance of this night’s sleep and the next day’s school dissolved into dust, I would—with a certain last-resortness, mind melting exhaustion, and boredom—leave my covers, open my laptop, put on a Bartok quartet, sit cross-legged on my bed, against my wall, and write whatever came to mind. In the deep darkness of my room, as my green walls morphed to grey, as my room became an isolated, onyx prism, a prison, cage of ethereal black bars—the only light that was was the screeching white glow of laptop unto face.

    And in those computer-white pages I could find a certain comfort. In the company of those words I was holding a mote of light, small, perhaps the size of a racquetball. It felt warm in my hand, round and soft, yet composed of an odd geometry: shapes that folded into themselves and each other, that rotated in planes beyond, with brilliance purer than any other, radiance otherworldly. It brought life to that liminal nullscape. It made those nights yet bearable. Perhaps something that truly mattered. I felt I could write the whole world.

—

    It was also during that time, of dreadconsciousness and life uncertainty, that I became aware of what I call now the whisper: a quiet voice that in answer to all the common questions of my circumstance then (what schools are you applying to?) spat back—in lieu of the mask of an answer my physical mouth would regurgitate—a plain, foggy phrase:

To be great.

    It wasn’t a burning desire that I chased with all of my being with all of a pronounced certainty. No, not that. It was just a whisper, a word. I couldn’t make sense of what this word great would mean in this context: it was simply a textual ideal that some quiet hunger within me, when I held that bit of light in the depths of the dark, told me I ought to be.

    It confused me, this fuzzy voice that sat in the corners of my head and that, even if I was unaware of it, was always watching me walk step by step through my life, examining my every thought, word, and action. And in moments when my mind drifted from the world around me and became briefly clear like crystal, I could then listen to this voice, who spoke not with words, but through soft notions: subtle longings and kernels of regret.

    I pointed the finger back at myself and came up with psychological faults for this voice’s residence in my head: greatness was just a cheap gambit for fame; hopes of being a great mathematician, musician, or writer were desires for my name to be in history books, in a wikipedia page translated into forty languages, or on the foot of a statue or overhang of a building, for many people of many times to laud over my achievements, to fill some hole within with the love of all the world.

    Or greatness was a cheap gambit for purpose, to extend the actions of my life beyond my short time on this earth, to, through my contributions to fields, movements, and lives, give my own some greater importance, to insert myself in an American history, a mathematical one, as a pioneering figure in some literary critic’s revolutionary chronology of cultural thought, that could be a meaning.

    But it wouldn’t be true; it would be a paltry one. Even if some great analyzer could deem me, my work as great, why is their word, that word, the one I live or die by? If to be great is to occupy the minds of those in the future who study the past, then is that greatness, your great life, your own? Are you, the author, the artist, the one who they shall write about? Or are you the decaying corpse in the ground? You die. You are destroyed. Your greatness will not be constructed by you, and instead, you will be constructed from it. Truth in self goes with the self, and any and all greatness I achieved would never be my own.

    It would not be for me.

    The whisperer’s words of greatness were no different from the words on how to live my life that the world spat at me every day: words of fortune, words of love, of exploration, of contentment, of nil. Choosing which words I listened to,

    Calculating which flavor of dust I liked best.

—

    …

    But that light.

    I recall that light.

    That mote I would hold in those moments when this world would crumble away, and what was was solely myself and the page—did it not gleam true?

    That light that I can catch glimpses of now: the color of the radiance, a white so brilliant it is iridescent; it is infinite inside—was its glow not real?

    The small mote leads to a world of luminosity: it feels like warmth, still air, beautiful clarity, gazing tear-eyed at eternity, and a quiet smile. Perhaps if the world were to sink into nothing, I would yet have this light, for at least when I hold it, I may know what it is I truly want.

    I want to write a masterpiece,

    To touch the souls of others as so much art in my life has touched mine.

    I know this light is truer than all else,

    For it has one thing behind it that nothing else has:


II. Goodness is a sunny breeze

    Over my first year in college I had found myself spouting, in answer to the common questions of my circumstance, phrases like “I’m going to stand in a wheat field in Nebraska”, “I’m going to herd sheep in Iceland”, or “I’m going to roam the steppes of Mongolia”.

    There was a certain lightness, an air in my step that I feel defined my psyche during that time: an open flat plain of possibility, like the places I would spout about, that underpinned my and probably my peers’ lives then. I interpret those answers now as verbal manifestations of that openness, that the first chapter of my life was behind me and that forever was in front of me—as perhaps truer responses than the answer that my mind via my mouth would often return: “probably math grad school or teaching.”

    Ever since those sleepless nights in my senior Spring when the green walls of my room would silently scream at me as my head caved in until I was a reduced to a husk that could only listen to Bartok string quartets and write nigh-schizophrenic ramblings on something called “greatness”, ever since I arrived to college and started smiling and waving and walking slowly—I thought that to be good was enough: to lead a good life, to be a good man. A new and growing part of me said that I ought to keep my head, to buy a tract of land and to tend it, to cultivate a space of beauty and serenity, a life of contentment and peace. It told the whisperer to quit with that nonsense of “greatness”, and that to, over one’s life, do right by oneself, others, and the world, is enough; we ought not to ask more of ourselves and our lives.

    It was this part that wanted to go stand in a wheat field in Nebraska.

    It was this part who filled my steps with air, gave my life that certain lightness, for I was becoming one with the breeze, a lone man standing in an open field, feeling the wind of a thousand acres swish and sweep past and through me. I knew what I wanted to be. I knew that I was almost there.

    I was performing my life’s Spring cleaning, neatly cordoning off its childhood chapter and tearfully letting go of the things I could no longer use, bittersweetly sealing away all those hockey games, midnight drives, basement hangouts, schooldays, and music lessons to live in the land of memory forever, in that orange-tinted portrait of a first year that sat in a cardboard box in the back of my Rav4, while I drove westward with that fresh, open feeling in my bones. I had left my life of agonizing in the dark, dreaming of greatness, and began to smile in the light, living with goodness. I felt holy and pure, not unlike a monk or an old man. I slept easy and long. I was present. I smiled and laughed and glowed so much, with so many others, also smiling, laughing, glowing. I developed a certain confidence, a confident certainty, one that was completely unfamiliar to but also felt right at home in my heart. At this stage in my life when everything was new, unformed, and yet to be, I felt a sense of surety that I hadn’t felt prior,

    And one that I perhaps haven’t felt since,

    For in that orange-tinted portrait, I had also sealed away, with the utmost care and scrutiny, that empty dread that would creep in the borders of my vision as I ate takeout alone, as I stared at the ceiling between rounds of a video game, and as I contemplated it all while gazing at the backs of my eyelids,

    And those nights when I would endlessly wander the green-tinged darkness of my room, looking for comfort in laptop docs, watching my face in the mirror grow foreign and vague.

    The portrait began to taint with blots of ink, stains that grew, that spread beyond the frame and to the box and the seat. It slithered, growing, across the doors, the windows; I-80 all around me—road, cars, trees, green signs—became instead a vast void. Pitch black manifold formed into the shape of my car. It might have still been driving but it didn’t matter because the outside world didn’t exist anymore. I couldn’t see beyond the walls. I was back in one of those sleepless nights in high school, back in my ethereal bedroom, me and my onyx prison.

    And thus, my psyche of elegance, sun, breeze, and fields flaked away piece by piece, like reversing a paper mache: the skin was falling off, bit by bit, slowly revealing what was underneath, exposing more and more until I looked in the mirror and I saw that I was that same high schooler who couldn’t sleep, who was afraid of the dark, and who would now and then fall away from this world and its delicious food, azure sky, and good people, and instead contemplate that pit that awaited him at the end of it all, drowning his ears in headphones and speakers—yet, at the same time too, listening to that old, soft voice deep in his heart that muttered, stated, chanted that same word infrequently but incessantly.

    The whisper of greatness came back,

    And thus, then on, with each passing week (each passing second), he relayed his growing unwillingness to wait for my will, through vague dissatisfactions and rumblings of urges that lived deep down. He told me I ought to be like Martin Luther (King Jr.), Joan of Arc, that I ought to be like Corrigan.

    And thus, once more, I wanted it all.

    That nagging thing within me ushered me to create. I felt I had a story to tell, a fusion of my consciousness with the current world and time it found itself in. I began dreaming of that light once more. I loved it. My wildest dreams: of capturing the experience of finding a way in this world as we all live in it, distilling it into words so poignant, so artful, vehicles of meaning that form into convoys and carry with them—through mechanisms so ingenious they elude all explanation, all analysis—raw feeling, raw experience, things so indescribable that they seem to come from outside and beyond this world; to, with writing, attempt to push on the limitations of our words, which can only describe our realities and never communicate them; to place line after line on the page, trying to build a bridge across this infinite chasm, trying to traverse the margin of error of human being. I wanted to tap into that light. I wanted to show it to all.

    For when I thought of greatness, I thought of those figures who changed my life—Tsukumizu, Natsume, and Yuasa; Bartok, Coltrane, and Stewart; and McCann, Kerouac, and Woolf—and I thought that that was what it meant to be great, that it was to, via art, convey impressions from the inexpressable divine, and that if I sought to be so and do such, then I had to fully accept my gift of this whispering friend, listen to him—he who recognized the gaps in our world, where goodness and light fall through into worldly suffering and nocturnal infinity—and work and strive to distill his solutions into pages, ceaselessly spreading it among followers, students, and friends, igniting a fire that burns all the possessive ills and suffocating incentive of our contemporarytechnologicalcapitalistic world and its taut strings and written scripts, leaving behind golden/green/turquoise beauty: sun whose rays shine on all, rocks that rise from stormy seas, cliffs that stare into infinity, fields of unthinkable breadth and majesty, and a nocturnal view of that warm star way off in the distance.

    The whisper had been trying to tell me for so long what I needed to do.

    He would show me the way,

    And all I needed to do was to put my faith in him, that he would guide me to the light,

    So long as I dedicated all to turn that faith into fact.


III. Epilogue

    I don’t hear that voice anymore, only the wind.

    The light’s so far away now.

    I can’t even see it behind the clouds.

    I’m here on this campus, standing, staring, off at the beautiful Maine sky, so vast, filled with fat long clouds, greyness stretching forever, a great big sheet that domes in these old brick buildings, these green-needled trees, and cold crisp air. The wind speeds through the land. It is massive. Bigger than anyone could ever be. Bigger than a city.

    I meld with the breeze: I become like another one of those green-needled trees, should one squint, subtly swaying.

    I no longer sleep like a baby. The insomnia that haunted my teenage years visits my new room every now and then. I’m still staring off, watching the wind, with all its might, slide the sky along only slowly, inch by inch, as it at the same time threatens to topple me, throw me away to some other place.

    So what could the light have been, then?

    The force of the wind and the weight of the sky are eroding me.

    It was like one of those long nights in high school, when nothing was real except the laptop, its music, and my writing.

    …

    I feel a compulsive urge to be entirely honest.

    I wear my malaise on my face and in my stride. I walk slowly with weight, dread, and fatigue. The only things I can hear are the wind and my headphones. I can’t stand to listen to anything else. The world is turning so fast (I have so much homework due). The sky is so gray.

—

    I’m in my room now. My roommate’s there. I don’t take off the headphones.

    I lay on my bed. I pick up a book for the first time in months.

    The pages speak a preacher’s words: of sinners, of the monument of the damnation of hell, its pitch-darkness, its eternity, and its being an apt punishment for those who turn towards lesser, base, animal instincts and turn away from their higher, divine, God-given rea—

    I’m taken out of the passage by the music. The lyric of song removes me from the lyric of sermon. I thrust myself once more unto the pages. Words and paragraphs feed into my eyes. Some minutes slip by. Some pages too. None enter my brain.

    I insert the bookmark, then close the book.

    I set it down, then close my eyes.

    …

    The music takes me away—no, it places me here: not where I am physically, not my bed, my room, but in the dim backstage behind the eyelid-curtain, what I am feeling, my consciousness feeling itself.

    I feel the music as I felt it the first time I listened to it, as I will in the future. Memories and moments live in the sound. Or maybe my memories sound like this music, and stages of my life sound like what I was listening to during them. Track by track, I invisibly drift along in pitch darkness, but I’m not moving: I just see colors, faint little radial pulses of blue and green, the red of the blood in my eyelids. Colors can be a sort of movement.

—

    The album ends.

    My eyes are still closed.

    Silence.

    …

    I sit up.

    I open my eyes to see what’s there.

    It’s my room, all the same, nothing strange.

    I take off my headphones, and I begin to hear once more the quiet music of this building: electronic vibrations, wind whistling, and bells tolling from far away.

    …

    The only thing I could think of doing next was writing about it all
