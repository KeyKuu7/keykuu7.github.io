Part I

    He stepped out of the car to stand on his jelly legs. And he took in for the first time in a while, with midday sun behind tristate haze, his neighborhood of identical, newly-fabricated homes—so close that they were connected, facades blended into one another, planted on the edge of a new-gen strip mall. They were constructed in a wavy, broken grid, such that each straight or rippling row of housing homogeneity was pointed in no uniform direction. The houses were all varying tones of gray.

    His parents weren’t home to greet him. He stood by the car for a minute, taut in some broad way, and took in with that anterior view of his house—distinguishable from those adjacent only by placarded numerals—those various reflections on home and school and friends and forbearance that had persistently bubbled up in his mind on that long drive back. Winding trees and drying air. He leaned against his car, feeling life seep back down into his legs, thwicking and flexing them a bit. He checked his phone. He didn’t want to start moving stuff in yet; he had just gotten home; he was tired; he deserved a bit of rest. He trudged up the stone steps to the door, coolly turning the key and entering.

    And a distance away, among a silent crowd of bags and duffels, back in the car, in the cupholder behind the gear shift, the half-drank Red Bull can stared sorrowfully at Thomas Sullivan, through the quarter-ellipse window frame, watching, with his entry, the house’s innermost glow be eclipsed by the heart-red door’s shutting.

    His phone was about to die. His brain felt like the consistency of ground beef. He peeled himself off the couch, firmly planting the first foot on the floor, pushing steps to the door. Summer afternoon miasma pressed into his pores. His charger was in his backpack. The car was a near-distant mirage, and he peered far through its window, searching. The line of his gaze found the Red Bull can, the first clothes duffel, and then the backpack wedged between middle seat and center console. He checked his phone; he ought to move it all in now; he had things to do. His head was foggy.

    He slung, crossways, both duffels on his shoulders and marched into the house. The car’s doors were left open for the unloading process, and it looked like an insect with its wings out. In the living room he paused, bags dropped footside, and stood still for a moment. His mind flickered. He checked his phone: he still had to charge it.

    Thomas was going to take shrooms tonight. He hadn’t done them since high school. He and three friends had planned it out over the last week or so: Thomas’s parents were going to be in the city at a dinner at the Yale Club and were staying with his cousin (once-removed), leaving his house unoccupied until noon the next day; Colin Zhang and Marty Karlsson were coming back from Cornell visiting one of their other friends and would get back around 11:00; and Thomas had arranged for 15 grams of dried gold cap psilocybin mushrooms to be placed in his mailbox at 7:00, tonight. As he passed back outside to grab his backpack and suitcase, gray, bubbling murmurs from the long drive spoke to him: he was plain and open and talked about everything with his friends from school; but he neglected that the beauty of old friends was of the vast catalogue of time and life that went fully unspoken, assumed, slumbering placidly under the floors of every basement gathering. And he thought about the trip: he was excited to take shrooms—sensory scrambling tinged with emotional honesty, good fun—but he also had been sober for the last two months: all of it. But shrooms weren’t addictive. (A dim fault was placed in the back of his head.) He lugged the suitcase out of the trunk and snatched his backpack out of the back seat. He shut all the car doors. He walked listlessly to the doorway once more. He’d get the can and any other trash on the next go.

    He added his backpack and luggage to his living room’s now considerable pile of bags. He slid out his phone’s charging brick and cable from his backpack’s front pocket, plugging them in in the outlet right by the couch, then connecting the phone. Obeying the confines of the cable, he let himself be dragged down to the couch to open his messages. Confirmation from the dealer. Marty and Colin would stop by their houses first, would be there ~11:20. Colin would bring his coffee scale for dosage. No word from Michael. Thomas swiped to his home page, watching all his app icons, vibrant little rounded squares, scroll seamlessly across the screen. He stared at his phone’s background: a picture of him and his parents on vacation in Geneva, the sun brilliantly clear, smiles brimming just as bright before sharp blue sky. There was a warm star somewhere unreachable in his head. It made him feel hollow. The photo was as lucid as the screen-glass he viewed it through, and it was only disturbed by a small, reflected glimmer from the living room lights—way up above—who saw Thomas on the couch: fetally sideways, held in abeyance by the device in his hand, face lit with an unnatural glow. A small colorful rectangle of light danced in the central, darkest portion of his eyes.

    And after some time, the unfinished Red Bull can turned its patience instead towards the windshield, gazing long towards the end of Thomas’s row of houses, a neighborhood planned, at the angry trees, fickle air, and prismatic sky, all tinged the quietest of greens by the great big Whole Foods sign, whose glow was made ever-prominent by the day’s weakening.

—

    He had found himself now supine, feeling intimately the pressure of his skull on his brain as well as waterfalls of mind-haze. A square of orange cream cola sky was captured by a window frame. He couldn’t look at his phone anymore and left it somewhere beside him. From the ceiling lights’ view, he looked like one of those crime scene chalk outlines, just splayed, belongings strewn. He was searching for something in the subtle pattern of the ceiling: the unevenness of paint, where the beams were, whether sheetrock was truly flat. Drywall. Dry walls. He thought of how he, Colin, and Marty had used to volunteer constructing homes in high school. It had been nice to spend some hours thinking of nothing more than 2x4s and hanging drywall and not using too much caulk. There was a tactility to it all. Thomas enjoyed the demolition days, using the sledgehammer, dialing in the swing: starting with the high left hand to get the whole thing started, accelerating, then, as it was arcing overhead, as each ounce of the head of the hammer’s carbon spring steel plummeted, millimeter by millimeter, the left hand slid down the handle, giving way, rotationally, to the now-birthed momentum of the handled beast, hurtling down towards whatever poor cabinet or cupboard awaited destruction. He found something about it immensely satisfying, pure in some way. Feeling the arc of mass carving through space. Marty muscled the thing too much. Colin’s arms were too thin. The three of them would volunteer on Sundays and were sometimes hungover, Thomas and Colin especially. Thomas realized he was starving. He rolled to his side, looking at the kitchen; he knew there was nothing in the fridge. Ordering food would take too long. He didn’t want to drive. Still, he slid off the couch, head still pounding, and filled a glass with his fridge’s water dispenser. The wimpy little stream took forever, and about a quarter-way through he wanted to check his phone but it was still plugged in by the couch. He felt the liquid hit the bottom of his stomach with an impact. It was past 7:00.

    The vast breadth of the orange sky was revealed to Thomas as he stepped out. Gray strand-clouds drew long lines across the sky, breaking it into sections. Summer stretched the day out, long—like the strand-clouds—until it became strange and orange like this, until at last night would have its turn, bathing the earth in warm dark. The mailbox stood in wait as Thomas approached, reticent. He flipped down the door and grabbed the shiny, vacuum-wrapped bag. The opacity of the bag’s sheen concealed some kind of essence. He would order a pizza for when they came. That would probably be enough. The oppressive sun was saying its last goodbyes, beginning to dip below the trees, still casting wide golden splatter onto the west side of the sky. The goods handheld, Thomas slid into the house, and, for the last time, the Red Bull can watched Thomas disappear behind the red door’s close.

—

    Scissors. Foil. Fungal dust. Thomas stood before his kitchen counter with these objects before him. They stood bold atop the pearl-white granite. The vacuum bag’s opacity had been cut open, breaking that fatal barrier, exposing its innermost contents: twenty or so unassuming dried mushrooms, sitting on a deep azure plate. He stared at the plate, at what it presented to him. His eyes glowed brown obsidian. Ever large. The shrooms were wrinkled, ribbed. He touched them. Like styrofoam. Alcohol was simply a chemical, a molecule. Weed was a plant, a leaf, an oil. But mushrooms were fungi, that strange in-between of plant and animal. They belonged to their own kingdom. They were fleshy. They communicated. And Thomas wondered: have they yet determined what they wished to do with him, his friends? He thought about his past trips: hiking in Vermont, screaming and tramping through wooded land; his family’s cabin Upstate, playing characters and talking their heads off—all touched with a childlike euphoria, visual vibrancy. And afterwards, there was this sense of relief: eternal, blissful relief. Relief from. And after the relief, he implicitly knew that he would not undergo that again for some months. They all did. It was beautiful but intense. They didn’t sit well in the stomach. Bile and fridge-water, mixing, sloshing.

    He was too tired to unpack. He opted to just lug the bags to his bedroom. Out of sight. He had returned to the couch, the phone: its glow remaining ever-bright as the orange one from the window dimmed to dull navy. The screen made his head hurt, made him feel like there was a crack in the back half of his brain. The phone provided glimpses into worlds outside of this house: sensory overload media remix hellscape, men and women claiming to have found the solution to male loneliness, fatal car accidents, assorted opinions on character and judgment, and assorted American university fraternities. The screen-glass window was one for the mind to escape through, to convince itself that the world around it is not so boring, and that passing the time can be just that much easier and quicker. As night descended lower, swelled fuller, the ceiling lights’ lucent stare circumscribed spotlights on the pigeon-gray shag, the hard maple table, and Thomas’s head, still gazing through.

    An odor radiated from the kitchen, like dreams, like death. A car was speeding down winding mountain parkways. Another lay dormant in a quiet, long driveway. Thomas’s sat outside with a vacant expression.

Part II

    11:22. Thomas sat in the mudroom, tapping his foot. It was completely dark outside, and thus, the windows of the house transformed from displays of the world beyond into reflective panes of black oil. But they stared in a way; that those outside may yet see the light and life within brings unease indoors. This is why curtains exist. So that the world may be blind to you so long as you are blind to it. The shrooms lay restlessly on the kitchen counter plate. Thomas stared at his socks, his cuticles. He got up and paced to look out the front window, searching for glints of light in the shimmering black. He hadn’t closed the curtains. There was no one to gaze into his house, no world to.

    Lustrous LED flood crawled forth outside, breaking asphalt’s gray. A car pulled to the curb. Faces inside. Thomas could see his reflection clearer than who was in the car. A tall Asian with short hair and a grey hoodie and dull sneakers, and a mildly-bearded youth in a wrinkled shirt with the collar all wrong as well as shorts. Knocks at the door.

    “Welcome lads.”

    “Where are my shroomies?” Marty said, rubbing his hands together with excessive arm motion.

    “How’s it going Thomas?” Colin said.

    “Good,” Thomas replied.

    They exchanged socially acceptable contemporary male handshakes and silently agreed to not engage in any more small talk for they were all too close for that.

    Colin pointed at the ground just by the door. “Should I just leave my shoes here?”

    “Yeah, that works,” Thomas responded. He began to walk. “I got them right here in the kitchen. On this plate.” Thomas pointed at the kitchen counter with the remnants of the foil wrapper, the scissors that made said wrapper into remnants, and the handful or so of mushrooms placed on that rather large blue plate. “Colin, did you bring the scale?”

    “Yeah.” Colin reached into the center of his hoodie pocket. He held the black scale in the air for a second, as if presenting to a crowd judging the object for authenticity, and gently, without much noise, placed the scale on the white granite, right by the blue plate.

    “You ready?” Marty asked.

    “I’m excited. It should be fun,” said Colin. He pointed to a high chair by the counter. Thomas nodded. He sat. “I was talking about this with Marty in the car, but Thomas, I want to hear your take. How would you describe shrooms?”

    “People usually compare it to weed,” Thomas replied. “It is like weed, but I also think it’s a lot like alc. You know how it makes you open, uninhibited? Shrooms kinda does that too. But it's a lot less crazy, calmer. But also it still gets you fucking high.” He was tilted over the counter, supporting his weight with his arms, outstretched, hands on the granite. “Crazy body sensations and visuals. You weren’t like seeing things or anything, but everything you looked at was vibrating, waving. Also your pupils get super fucking dilated. You’ll see.”

    “Alright, man, trying to be different, comparing it to alc,” Marty said. “It’s not that deep. Think weed but you're less stupid but the sensations are like ten times stronger.” The three of them were standing around in the kitchen. Marty turned to Thomas. “Don’t give him too many expectations going in. All he’s gotta know is that he’ll have a good time. Less you think, the better.” Marty plopped himself on the softest chair in the vicinity.

    “You guys know I’m not the type to tweak out. I don’t get in my head about my senses being jumbled. ‘Cause that’s what I think gets you. Saw it with some of my friends on weed,” Colin said.

    “Yeah, no, it’ll be good,” Thomas said. “How was Cornell, the drive?”

    “Long. It was cool to see how our boy’s been out upstate,” Colin said. “It’s a pretty place. Bear mountain parkway’s a lotta fun to drive. Shame we got back so late.”

    “We’re gonna be up all fuckin’ night off these guys!” Marty exclaimed.

    “Yeah, wait, so, how does that work?” Colin asked. “Like are we gonna feel tired but just can’t sleep? Or—?”

    “You just will not want to sleep,” Thomas replied. “You’ll feel fine. Maybe we’ll get tired at the end. But you won’t mind. You simply just stay awake.”

    “Yeah, basically what he said,” Marty said, tapping a fast beat on the chair’s arm rest. “It’s fucking crazy that we’re done with our first year. Do we feel like changed men? Like men at all?”

    “I feel pretty different, changed,” Colin said. “It’s been really great living on my own. I’m still kind of doing the same stuff, but I feel more engaged, everything’s more serious, in a good way. Still just classes and viola and guitar. I started playing ping pong though.” His chin sat heavy in his palm.

    “It’s been good. I still don’t know what I want to study yet. I’ve started climbing a lot more. A lotta people do it at my school,” Thomas said.

    “Way out in wherever-the-fuck Pennsylvania,” Marty quipped. Thomas made a face somewhere between a smile and eternal resignation. “I just fucking love philosophy man. Shit’s so fun. I fucking hate Boston in the winter.” He was bouncing his knee up and down, darting his eyes across the space: fridge, plate, mushrooms, TV, Thomas, window. The chair he was sitting in was plush and camel. “We’re right on the Charles so I’m just getting fucking stabbed in the face everytime I walk outside. Didn’t get good till like late April. Though I can’t wait to take quantum physics next semester.”

    “Oh yeah, I took a physics class this semester. I lowkey hated it,” Colin said. “We’re a smaller school so they stuff all the pre-meds and physics prospectives in the same two intro classes so that both fucking hate it.”

    “Damn that’s gotta suck,” Marty said.

    “Yeah. I might minor in it, stick out a few more, but I’ll probably just stay more with math. More fun,” Colin said.

    Thomas leaned himself back upright and moved next to the shrooms plate. “I’m gonna start measuring out now. How much are we taking?”

    “Yeah, wait, how much do you guys usually do?” Colin asked.

    “Let’s do like two grams each. Maybe a little more. We usually do around that,” Marty replied. “Oh yeah, where’s Michael? He hasn’t texted anything.”

    “Yeah, I don’t know.” Thomas glanced at the front door. “I got fifteen grams, so even with him, we lowkey got a lot. I can save some for myself, but do you guys maybe wanna do like two and a half each?”

    “I’ll do like 2.25 grams. 2.5 might be pushing it. First time after all,” Colin said. Something caught his eye, a reflected light in the window.

    Marty nodded. “Yeah that’s probably good. I’m down to do two and a half.”

    Thomas placed a small dish on the scale and began measuring the first dose. “Dope.”

    Knocks at the door.

    “Right on fucking time,” Marty said. “Classic Michael.”

    Thomas went to the door and opened it. There Michael Pickford stood—his, Colin’s, and Thomas’s cars lying dormant in a row behind him. Stocky in sweats and slides.

    “Bro, we thought you might not be coming. You airing us in the group chat,” Marty lightly shouted from his soft chair.

    “Hey fuck you man,” Michael replied, holding his expression flat.

    “Fuck you too,” Marty said, smiling.

    “Good to see you man, you guys.” Michael made his way around the space, sharing various hand joinings with Thomas at the door, Colin at the kitchen counter, and Marty in the big chair. He took a seat on the couch near Marty, moving the stray charging cable out of the way.

    Thomas closed the front door, made his way back to the counter. He began to present plates with measured, miniscule helpings of the shrooms. The varying types of plates, as well as the small portion of the plates the shrooms occupied, made it seem like Colin was a solo restaurateur at a high-end, reserve-months-in-advance kind of place, and that Thomas was his chef for the night. Kitchen counter with hanging lights overhead. The shrooms looked lifeless, like corpses on their plates. Thomas finished preparing the fourth dish and handed the scale to Colin. “You got here just in time. Got the doses measured and everything. Right here on these plates.”

    “Sick,” Michael replied.

    Colin stood up in his chair, leaning over the counter. “So which one’s mine? The 2.25?”

    Michael and Marty were chopping it up about girls and drinking and all that seemed to define the former’s college experience. It wasn’t really true for either of them, but it was what they tended to talk about. Thomas opened the door once again, handed the similarly aged lanky white dude in a too-large golf polo a small tip, and took the pizza box in, guiding the door shut with this foot. Colin was turning in his high chair watching both ordeals.

    Thomas placed the box on the empty part of the counter, away from the elaborate fungal affair. “Yeah, just start digging in.” He pointed at a cabinet up above near the stove. “More plates up there. You can also use your shroom plates.”

    Marty got up and shuffled over to the counter. Michael did the same after a short delay. The four of them were congregated around the great-white kitchen rectangle, a stage with foil, scissors, plates, fungi, cardboard, and pizza atop.

    “How much do I gotta pay you?” Colin asked.

    “Pizza’s on me. Just give me like twenty bucks each of you for the shrooms,” Thomas said.

There were only four entities in this house. Night gazed through the windows of this house in its lonely neighborhood. Fresh concrete makes a place look strange. The strip mall was full of it. Thomas’s house was the color of concrete and asphalt and andesite. Off-white stained the inner walls, and black glass eyes peered inwards, looking, looking along with the ceiling, kitchen, and lamp lights, at those four young men gathered around a pearl-white granite slab, and, particularly, at Thomas Sullivan.

Part III

    The four men were placed in a single scene, seated all around the hard maple table: Marty in his camel throne, Michael in a smaller, also singular chair, and Colin and Thomas on opposite ends of a smooth but not shiny leather three-seater. Michael was engrossed by his phone, thumbs hammering, expression positive. He had co-opted Thomas’s charger for his own use, and his enlarged, dark pupils focused on the center of the device, seeking to look through it, past it, though utterly unable. The empty pizza box sat that distance away atop the kitchen countertop, watching the scene from its vantage point. Colin was fully reclined, neck rolled over the top of the couch’s backrest, facing the solid sky. Marty was curled up like a dog or cat in the chair. They were placed in a semicircle, enclosing some 180 degrees around the invisible, ovalish arc of air that surrounded the center table. Thomas was staring at the walls, and his bones felt like they were melting. His pupils were huge. Glossy and dark.

    Colin’s upward-oriented face began to speak. “This shit is hitting.”

    “Yeah,” Thomas replied. Words seemed to fail at describing whatever sensory assault they were facing. They were not speaking much. The four of them looked in their chairs, set amongst each other, like they had been there their whole lives, like that was where they were always meant to be.

    “Michael, why are you always on your fucking phone?” Marty asked.

    “Hold up, I’m texting this girl,” Michael said, wide eyes still transfixed. “Also, if I look away and start seeing the walls move again I’m gonna fucking lose it.”

    “From school?” Marty asked. “Bro, we’re here for the whole summer. What are you doing texting some girl halfway across the country?”

    “I’m plotting bro.”

    “Focus on the trip, your friends. Wavy walls and shit is part of it all,” Marty said. “Ride along. Spiritual journey. Mushrooms.”

    Michael didn’t respond. Colin rocked forward and sat normally. Thomas noticed and turned his head towards him. “The walls are fucking crazy,” Thomas said.

    “Yeah,” Colin replied.

    The paint on the walls and the lights up above and the door in the distance stood ever still. They stood and stared and watched. They enclosed this space and saw four men sprawled on couches and chairs sinking ever deeper into them. Marty was lightly rolling. Colin fell to his side, head resting on the big wide arm rest of the couch. Michael sat unchanged. Thomas got up and began to step.

    “Woah, woah, woah,” Marty exclaimed. “You’re disrupting the scene. You can’t move. You’ll leave a gap in the couch, in the energy.”

    Thomas turned back towards Marty. “I just wanna walk around the kitchen for a second. I haven’t stood up since my legs turned to Jell-O. I wanna feel myself step.”

    Colin got up as well. “I’m gonna take a piss.”

    Marty sat up erect. “No, no. You guys are breaking the arrangement. We were seated here perfectly.” Colin walked to the bathroom by the front door. “We were seated in a semicircle. You can’t leave all that empty space on the couch.”

    “It’ll just be a second,” Thomas replied to Marty’s pleading face poking out from his chair’s side.

    The four of them were now strung throughout the house. Thomas stood like he did before, hands on the countertop, leaning. Michael moved from his chair to take over the couch, laying flat on his back, mouth softly open staring at the device. Colin was washing his hands in the powder room, staring at himself in the mirror. His eyes were jet. Onyx. Giant. Marty curled tighter in the chair, almost writhing. The nighttime was oppressive. The men became creatures, though this was not an unpleasant thing, rather the opposite. Marty closed his eyes slightly, seeing the lights dance behind his eyelids. Thomas stared at the grease-stained pizza box’s bottom and the fridge water dispenser and the red front door. Michael had stopped texting and was now scrolling through various videos, worlds. Colin was still at the powder room mirror, inspecting his irises: how they had blended with his pupils, how when he stared at them too long he would teeter and lose his footing because he was leaning too far forward. These were not inherently joyful states, but because they were nothing more than simple, describable states, the men found an innocent joy in their simplicity, their plainness. Their experiences’ honesty. Experience’s honesty.

    Michael giggled. He put down his phone. Thomas stared straight through the wall ahead of him, but he could not see beyond the house, he would never, at least not now. The black of night was a mere backdrop for the house’s existence. There were stairs leading up, and stairs going down to some fuzzy darkness. The four had made an agreement at some point prior to not go down, for the visual image of losing one man to a descent into darkness would be too much for an altered mind, and would spell doom and fear immeasurable, even if that same man would come back up some seconds later, seemingly unchanged. Thomas looked down. The darknesses in the centers of his eyes were massive, like small black holes, black pearls. Colin was pondering his experience, his sensations; Marty was romanticizing them; Michael was swept up by them. But Thomas simply gazed at the wall ahead of him, at Marty on the chair, and Michael on the couch. His stomach felt like a stone, and his head rippled in waves, waves that broke and sailed and crashed against the walls of his skull, his mind. Against the cracks as well. Still, what he was doing with the sensations and experience at hand, was not known, was not given. Thomas seemed more or less the same whether he was alone or with people, on drugs or not, at school or home. He was and always would be Thomas Sullivan. He walked back to the couch.

    Thomas stood over Michael for a second, sending an inaudible message. After seven seconds, Michael understood, and brought himself up to a sitting pose. Thomas sat.

    Marty’s eyes were open. He was sideways on his chair, looking at Thomas. “Do you think Colin’s losing his shit in there?”

    “I don’t know. Maybe.” Thomas looked towards the door and shouted, “Colin, you alright?”

    “Yeah,” cried a distant voice.

    “Guess he’s chilling,” Marty said.

    “This shit fucks with our sense of time. I bet he’s only been in there for like two minutes,” Thomas said.

    “But it’s felt like so long.” Marty sniffed. “Welcome back to the scene, though. I’m glad you’ve decided to mend your part of our beautiful, positional arrangement. We just need fucking Colin back.”

    Thomas grinned only lightly. “I think I’m starting to get your obsession with ‘the scene’ now. The empty chair where Colin’s gonna sit feels wrong being empty. Like there needs to be a person there. Us sitting here is missing that.”

    Colin stepped out of the bathroom. Marty and Thomas were gesturing energetically for him to sit back down. Colin walked over only slowly, causing Marty and Thomas to accelerate their gesturing. Colin finally sat on Michael’s old chair. Michael looked at Marty with a fat grin.

    Pizza grease soaked deep into cardboard. Mushroom dust sat patiently on kitchen plates. LEDs buzzed. Walls waved. The door remained locked, and the four young men sat in their same semicircle around that hard maple living room table, their arrangement simply permuted, as the cars sat outside in a line, the Red Bull can sat in its cupholder, and the night’s newest stars sat suspended in that navy-black blanket looking upon those small, gray houses in that strip mall, and the Whole Foods sign held only a soft, dark green as it too, sat, now unilluminated, its color lying only on its surface, not beyond. Thomas Sullivan’s house’s front had an expression of wonder, wondering.

Coda

    “Do you guys wanna walk around?” Marty asked. “I wanna walk around.”

    “Yeah,” Thomas said. Colin also nodded.

    “Bro, I’m too fucked,” Michael said. He was laying across the couch again. Thomas and Colin had moved to the kitchen counter. The scene had been broken at some point. “I just wanna lay here.”

    “Maybe we’ll catch some of it.”

Thomas unlocked the red front door, and turned his head to look at Michael all the way on the couch: eyes closed, with a disquieted expression. He held that image in his mind for just another moment, and then the three of them stepped out. The door shut.

    “Time doesn’t feel real,” Colin said.

    “Yeah. I remember when we were fucking bugging out between like two and three and it felt so slow. Now we’re kind of going along, normally,” Marty said. His hands were jammed in his shorts pockets. His arms were bone-straight. Locked.

It was fun, we were giggling, had the tingles, the visuals, but I feel free in like a higher way. It’s like when you drink and you’re uninhibited and fucking crazy and shit. And when you drink you sometimes say and do shit you wouldn’t normally say or do. But for this, it’s like your impulses are restrained by your character. You feel unrestricted, but you also feel honest and true, to yourself, you know?”

There’s something beautifully liminal about this damned shopping center when it’s dark like this, and there’s no one around, no cars, no lights. It’s just the pavement and the parking lines.”

    “It’ll be twilight soon,” Marty said. He was leading the pack. He stopped for a second. He pointed. “Do we wanna go this longer way down those stairs to that parking lot? Or do we wanna go straight to the Whole Foods for our loop?”

Long ass night.” The three were closing in on the house, on its gray. The sun had not yet risen. The buildings were indistinguishable from the sky.

    “Hey, it’s our cars,” Thomas said. The three vehicles were in line at the curb. Thomas’s was first, followed by Colin’s, then Michael’s. They were dutifully arranged before the house’s front steps, like royal guards, and inside the first car, Thomas’s, in the cupholder behind the gearshift, the old, wistful, half-drank Red Bull can awoke, hearing distant voices outside. It gazed once more through that quarter-ellipse window to see what was outside. It saw three boys, or men, walking, walking solemnly, tranquilly, loosely, and perhaps openly. One was tall, and had a short layer of black hair on his head, as well as a loose gray hoodie, black cargo pants, and dull sneakers. Another was a more modest height, and wore a short-sleeved collared shirt, blue with white shapes patterning it, wrinkled, its collar a mess—along with beige shorts and white leather sneakers. And the third, Thomas Sullivan (it could see him clearly this time), had a bulb of tawny hair, a green T-shirt that read in faded orange and white text ‘Ireland,’ and ripe blue jeans. His eyes were brown. His face was mildly freckled. And his expression was open. The trio were approaching the car, the can. Or maybe they were just approaching the house. The Red Bull can continued to watch Thomas, looking at him, through him, searching for something in the center of his pupils. The can stared as Thomas walked past the car, towards his house’s face, his expression now hidden, obscured.

    “Hold on guys, I’m just gonna grab this can from my car to throw out.”

The three stood before the house’s inner light once more, perhaps frozen, perhaps taking a breath, for a mere second—no, less than that, a mere moment, before stepping inside, closing the door behind them. The day was shaking off its sleeping dust once again. Yellow rays began to reach the top, the center of the sky. That great gradient of the deepest violet and dandelion yellow formed a sphere, a dome that foreclosed on it all: Thomas’s house, its heart-red door, the three cars, the Whole Foods, the strip mall—and things began to stir. An employee was unchaining shopping carts. A man started his car for work. A sedan pulled up to the Starbucks drive-thru for morning coffee. The sun could begin to be made out behind the hills and trees of this wooded land. Tristate haze had been lifted for but a single night, a night so clear it shimmered. Now, day was to come once again, once more. A bird chirped, and the parkway nearby began its low roar. Sun’s radiant vectors shot through the wood, and the Whole Foods sign stood proud. The asphalt lot and concrete sidewalks gobbled up the new day’s rays, and the moon hung still visible, now stripped of its chilling glow, now only a distant, concrete-gray rock, a piece suspended in the sky. With each passing second, like falling tears or gentle pacing, a new cone of sun would come out from behind the tree-lined hills, falling on something cool and dark. One would fall on a white parking line. Another on a flowering bush. One more on a black wire chair, and, another on a plane glass window, on a gray indistinct house, into a living room, to a couch, on a sleeping, stocky man in sweats whose face wore fear, whose air held pain, whose lips were shut tight like a seam, and whose body was splayed far and curled tight at the same time. He looked uncomfortable, disturbed. His name was Michael Pickford, and he was sound asleep.
